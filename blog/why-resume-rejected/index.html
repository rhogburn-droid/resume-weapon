Why Is My Resume Getting Rejected? 10 ATS Mistakes Killing Your Applications

Updated March 2026 · 7 min read

You're qualified for the role. You've tailored your cover letter. You hit submit and hear nothing back. If this happens over and over, the problem likely isn't you — it's your resume format. Specifically, it's how applicant tracking systems (ATS) are reading (or failing to read) your resume before a human ever sees it.

Studies show that up to 75% of resumes are rejected by ATS software before reaching a recruiter. Here are the 10 most common reasons — and how to fix each one.

1. You're using a creative template with columns and graphics

Two-column layouts, sidebar designs, icons, and infographic-style resumes look great to humans but confuse ATS parsers. The software reads left-to-right, top-to-bottom, and columns cause content to merge or disappear entirely. Text inside images, charts, or graphics is completely invisible to ATS.

Fix: Use a single-column layout with standard formatting. Save the creative design for your portfolio, not your resume file.

2. Your resume doesn't match the job description's keywords

This is the number one reason qualified people get filtered out. ATS software scans for specific terms from the job posting — exact skills, certifications, tools, and qualifications. If the posting says "project management" and you wrote "managing projects," the ATS may not match them.

Fix: Read the job posting carefully and mirror its exact language in your resume. Use the same terms, acronyms, and phrases the employer used.

3. You're submitting the wrong file format

Some ATS platforms can't properly parse certain file types. PDFs with complex formatting, .pages files, or image-based files often cause problems. While modern ATS handles standard PDFs well, .docx remains the safest universal format.

Fix: Submit as .docx unless the posting specifically asks for PDF. If you must use PDF, ensure it's text-based (you should be able to select and copy text from it).

4. Your section headers are too creative

ATS software looks for standard section labels to categorize your information. Headers like "My Journey," "Where I've Excelled," or "The Toolbox" won't be recognized. The software literally can't find your work experience if the section isn't labeled as such.

Fix: Use standard headers: Professional Experience, Education, Skills, Summary, Certifications. Boring works.

5. You're missing a dedicated skills section

Many ATS platforms scan a dedicated skills section separately from your experience bullets. If your technical skills are only mentioned within job descriptions but not listed in a standalone section, the ATS may not register them as core competencies.

Fix: Add a "Core Skills" or "Technical Skills" section listing your key competencies, tools, and technologies — especially the ones mentioned in the job posting.

6. Your dates and job titles are formatted inconsistently

ATS needs to parse your employment timeline. Inconsistent date formats (mixing "Jan 2023" with "2023-01" with "January 2023"), unclear date ranges, or missing dates entirely can cause parsing errors that make your experience look disjointed.

Fix: Use one consistent date format throughout. "Month Year – Month Year" (e.g., "Jan 2022 – Mar 2024") is the most universally parsed format.

7. You're using text boxes, tables, or headers/footers

Content placed in Word text boxes, table cells, or document headers and footers is often ignored by ATS parsers. Your name and contact info in a header? The ATS might not find it. Skills listed in a table? Potentially invisible.

Fix: Put all content in the main document body. No text boxes, no tables for layout, no header/footer content.

8. You're not spelling out acronyms

Some ATS search for the full term, others search for the abbreviation. If you only write "SEO" but the job posting says "Search Engine Optimization," you might miss the match — or vice versa.

Fix: Include both forms on first use: "Search Engine Optimization (SEO)." This covers both search methods.

9. Your resume is too long (or too short)

One-page resumes with minimal content may not contain enough keywords to score well. Conversely, five-page resumes with excessive detail can dilute your keyword density and overwhelm the parser. Both extremes hurt your ATS score.

Fix: Aim for 1-2 pages for most roles, 2-3 for senior positions. Include enough detail to hit key terms but stay focused on relevant experience.

10. You're sending the same resume to every job

This is the most common mistake and the easiest to fix. Every job posting emphasizes different skills, uses different terminology, and prioritizes different qualifications. A generic resume will only match a fraction of what any specific ATS is scanning for.

Fix: Tailor your resume for each application. It doesn't mean rewriting from scratch — adjust your skills section, summary, and bullet point language to mirror each specific posting.
